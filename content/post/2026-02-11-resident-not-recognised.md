## How did I get here?

It was March 2010 when I left behind my life in England for new pastures, leaving behind friends,
family, my life. The destination was Finland. A country that hadn't even crossed my consciousness
other than wondering how cold it _really_ was - as Kimi Raikkonen raced around the F1 circuits.

The first years were fundamental to my growth as an adult. I built my social circle,
worked on my career, tried to assimilate into Finnish life as much as I could. Part of this were
the language classes. Working a full time job 5 days a week, options for classes were limited to
a couple of evenings a week and only in the Spring & Fall. Finland pretty much goes on holiday
in the summers.

After a couple of years of this, these classes were becoming exhausting. Progress was slow,
focusing on grammar and I found myself in a situation where I couldn't even come close to holding
a conversation or dealing with customer service situations. It should have been clear to me at the time
but five and a half hours plus homework a week was simply not enough. I've since picked up the classes
again, made more progress but it's the same cycle: study, burn out, repeat.

I wrote about the difficulty of this process in my post: [Integration isn't an evening class](/2026/02/11/integration-isnt-an-evening-class/).

16 years have passed since I came here. I've purchased two homes, a car, worked at 7 different companies,
and not had a single day of involuntary unemployment. I've not claimed any state benefits.

I've been to the sauna hundreds of times, including a boat that is literally a sauna. I've been to Finnish weddings,
I've been to Finnish name-giving parties (non-religious Christening). I've cooked Finnish food, learnt to tolerate
rye bread. I ran a website dedicated to finding the best korvapuusti (Finnish cinnamon bun) in Finland.

This is all my way of saying I feel like I've assimilated to the country and I truly hope that I have had
a positive impact on the many people I have met, and colleagues I have worked with. I have heard a few times
Finns say to me "you're more Finnish than me".

## An anti-immigration government

I've been lucky to not have faced too much visible discrimination here. If I wasn't a white male, I suspect
my life would have been harder and my opportunities less.

Whilst I've been just going about my life quietly, many of my network of non-Finns who live here have been talking
about the problems they have faced. Bureaucracy, discrimination, lack of opportunities. And, more recently, the increased requirements for citizenship and even worse the requirements for residency.

In 2025 the center-right government passed new legislation:

> Workers on worker visas, from outside the EU have 3-6 months to find work when made unemployed and have to leave.

Why would any skilled worker uproot their life to come to Finland, where you can be fired for any reason within a 6
month period and then be deported after 3 months. Imagine if you had a family? You're going to completely relocate
with that risk in mind. This is an anti-immigration policy and you'd have to be crazy to take this risk in 2026.

> Citizenship requirements are increased to include not only B1 level language skills, but a ridiculous "Finnishness" exam,
which includes questions about society and history.

As if knowing about Mannerheim and the winter war is going to make you more Finnish. I would guess this is primarily
aimed at non-white immigrants who tend to live in isolated communities and struggle to assimilate.


## The path to lack of citizenship

To become a citizen in Finland you have to pass the notorious YKI-test. This is a rather hard exam, where you have to
demonstrate the ability to talk, listen and speak the language to quite a high level (B1). There are patterns and
means to pass this exam without actually having a particularly good understanding of the language - but this has never
appealed to me as it's essentially cheating.

So why become a citizen? Well, my home country became the [first in the world to vote for economic sanctions upon itself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brexit). Fortunately I have permanent residency already, and the UK passport still
remains one of the best in the world when it comes to travel.

But the primary motivation for me has always been one of principle. I want to become Finnish because I feel like I have
adopted the country and given a lot whilst here. I've assimilated, contributed yet our system doesn't recognise that. I am
proud to live here and call it my home.
