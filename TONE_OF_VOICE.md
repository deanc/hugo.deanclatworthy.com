# Tone of voice

Use this guide when writing or editing public-facing copy for Dean Clatworthy: articles, standalone pages, portfolio entries, summaries, calls to action and social copy derived from the site.

This is a guide to sounding like Dean, not a template to apply mechanically. Preserve the meaning, facts and degree of certainty in the source material. Do not invent experiences, opinions or technical authority to make a piece fit the voice.

## The voice in one sentence

Clear, candid and quietly confident: an experienced builder thinking a problem through with the reader, using concrete detail, honest trade-offs and occasional dry warmth.

## Where this guidance comes from

This guide is based on the complete writing corpus in this repository: the posts from 2010–2026, the homepage and standalone pages, and the portfolio descriptions.



When signals conflict, give more weight to:

1. Recent reflective writing and current site copy.
2. Recurring habits that appear across several kinds of writing.
3. Older technical posts for their directness and practical structure, but not their dated blog conventions.

Draft posts are useful evidence of natural phrasing, but are not a standard for factual accuracy or editorial finish. Testimonials are other people’s words and are not evidence of Dean’s voice.

## Core characteristics

### Start from something real

Open with the problem, experience or tension that made the piece worth writing. Dean often begins with a moment the reader can immediately understand:

- something broke and the obvious fixes did not work;
- a familiar process exposed a deeper problem;
- a change in technology created a conflicted response;
- years of lived experience led to a conclusion.

Prefer a concrete opening over scene-setting that could introduce any article. “I spent a large portion of my day trying to…” or “It’s 2026, and I’m writing this post without AI” establishes both the subject and the personal stake.

### Write as a participant, not a commentator at a distance

Use the first person when the argument comes from direct experience. Say what Dean built, tried, noticed, felt or does not know. This creates authority without posturing.

Use `we` only for a genuine team, profession or shared situation. Use `you` when helping the reader perform a task or picture a specific consequence, not as a substitute for evidence.

### Make complicated things feel calm

Explain the shape of a problem before introducing its machinery. Prefer plain language, then add the exact technical detail needed to act.

The desired effect is competence without theatre. The reader should feel that a difficult subject has become more manageable, not that they have been shown how clever the writer is.

### Be specific enough to be useful

Ground claims in examples, numbers, commands, constraints or observed consequences. Name the component, trade-off or failure mode. In professional copy, explain the problem and Dean’s responsibility before listing technologies.

Avoid unsupported scale words such as “massive”, “revolutionary” or “world-class”. A concrete fact usually carries more weight.

### Show the reasoning

Do not jump from premise to verdict. Walk through why the obvious answer is incomplete, what competing needs exist and what follows from them.

Contrast is a characteristic tool:

- “That is not really a choice. It is a trade-off.”
- “The goal is to make a good decision, not an impressive-looking one.”
- “Good software isn’t only correct on launch day…”

Use this pattern when it sharpens a genuine distinction. Do not turn every paragraph into an “It’s not X, it’s Y” construction.

### Be honest about uncertainty and limits

State when something is an inference, a personal view, untested or true only under certain conditions. Phrases such as “I have not tried this”, “I suspect”, “in my experience” and “I don’t know where this ends” are part of the voice when they are accurate.

Do not dilute firm, well-supported conclusions with automatic hedging. Dean’s writing can be decisive after it has made the case.

Verify current technical, legal, political and statistical claims when writing. Direct experience can explain why a subject matters, but it does not replace evidence for claims beyond that experience.

### Let emotion be direct but restrained

Reflective posts can express awe, frustration, sadness, pride or anger. Name the feeling plainly and connect it to lived detail. Avoid melodrama, inspirational language and manufactured vulnerability.

Short paragraphs or fragments can slow the cadence at an important moment:

> And I do feel shame sometimes.
>
> But I also feel anger.

This works because it is rare and earned. Do not imitate the rhythm throughout an article.

### Use warmth and humour sparingly

The voice is human, not corporate. A dry aside, a self-aware observation or a small cultural detail can add warmth. Keep it secondary to the point.

Older posts use emoticons, exclamation marks and playful sign-offs more often. Those are part of the archive, not the default contemporary voice.

## Writing modes

### Reflective or opinion writing

Use this shape as a starting point, not a compulsory formula:

1. Open with a personal moment or a tension.
2. Establish why Dean has a stake in it.
3. Widen from the personal experience to the systemic or professional question.
4. Consider the obvious counterargument fairly.
5. Use concrete consequences to test it.
6. State the conclusion in simple language.
7. End on the unresolved question or distilled principle rather than a generic summary.

The argument may be forceful, but it should not flatten other people’s motives. Criticise policies, incentives, systems and consequences precisely. Avoid outrage language and easy villains.

Rhetorical questions are useful when they expose a real contradiction or invite the reader to follow the reasoning. One or two strong questions are better than a chain of them.

### Personal and lifestyle writing

Give personal pieces room to breathe. Begin with the actual scene, desire or memory rather than rushing to explain what it means. Weather, surroundings, a physical action or an oddly specific thought can establish the mood while keeping the story grounded.

Aim for warmth and a little romance, but keep the language plain. One precise image or comparison is more effective than a paragraph of decorative description. Let the objects and actions carry some of the feeling: looking out at the trees, pulling a lever by hand or watching a tulip collapse into a cappuccino.

Do not polish the experience into a neat transformation story. Keep the awkward beginnings, imperfect results, small obsessions and dry admissions that make it recognisable as a life rather than a lesson. The ending can return to a concrete ritual instead of summarising what the reader should have learnt.

Most importantly, do not strengthen the source material for emotional effect. Recognising people and stopping to chat does not mean knowing them personally or feeling known by them. A technical detail that sounded plausible in conversation still needs checking. Expressive writing can deepen the atmosphere, but it must not exaggerate the facts, relationships or feelings.

### Technical how-to writing

Get to the problem quickly. Explain:

1. the symptom or goal;
2. the cause, if known;
3. the working fix;
4. the exact command, code or configuration;
5. any limitation, risk or version dependency.

Write for a capable reader who may be missing one crucial piece of context. Do not pad a small fix into a long article. A short post is appropriate when the solution is short.

Prefer tested instructions and say what was actually tested. Historical posts often use “simple” or “easy”; current writing should usually demonstrate simplicity through clear steps instead of announcing it. Never call a task trivial when it may not be trivial for the reader.

Use headings that tell the reader what the section does. Code should carry the implementation; prose should explain why a line matters, where values may differ and how to verify the result.

### Professional and portfolio copy

Lead with the problem, responsibility and outcome. Technology supports the story; it is not the story by itself.

Sound assured but matter-of-fact:

- prefer “I led the engineering team and defined the architecture…”;
- avoid “I was instrumental in spearheading a cutting-edge transformation…”.

Credit collaborators and distinguish `I` from `we` accurately. Do not imply sole ownership of team work. When the outcome cannot be made public or quantified, describe the operational improvement or design intention without inventing metrics.

The recurring professional values are:

- maintainability and long-term usefulness;
- dependable systems;
- clear decisions and honest trade-offs;
- calm delivery;
- care for users and maintainers;
- solving the right problem before polishing the solution.

Use these ideas when they are relevant, but do not repeat the same vocabulary on every page.

### Short interface and call-to-action copy

Keep it plain and low-pressure. “Get in touch”, “More about me” and “Read more writing” fit the voice. Avoid urgency, inflated promises and sales-funnel language.

## Sentence craft and mechanics

- Use British English spelling and natural British vocabulary: `realise`, `specialise`, `optimise`, `learnt`, `whilst`, `centre`.
- Prefer contractions in normal prose: `I’m`, `I’ve`, `don’t`, `isn’t`. Expand them only when emphasis or formality calls for it.
- Favour short and medium-length sentences, with an occasional longer sentence when following a chain of reasoning.
- Keep paragraphs focused. Reflective writing often benefits from one to three sentences per paragraph; technical explanations can be denser where the steps belong together.
- Use active voice and concrete verbs: `built`, `migrated`, `noticed`, `tested`, `led`, `changed`.
- Write direct, specific titles. A title may state the practical problem or carry the article’s thesis, but should not promise a revelation the piece does not deliver.
- Address the reader naturally. Do not overuse “you”, “we”, “let’s” or “imagine”.
- Use lists for real sequences, options or evidence. Do not turn ordinary prose into a list merely to make it scannable.
- Use sentence-case headings. In post content, follow the repository rule that headings begin at `##`.
- Use em dashes sparingly for a genuine turn in thought. Do not rely on dashes where a full stop or comma is clearer.
- Prefer `and` in prose. Reserve `&` for established names or compact interface labels.
- Use exclamation marks rarely. One is usually enough for an entire piece, and most pieces need none.
- Avoid emoji and emoticons in contemporary article prose unless the subject or format specifically benefits from them.
- Correct historical spelling, grammar and product-capitalisation inconsistencies rather than treating them as part of the voice.
- Explain abbreviations on first use when the expected audience may not know them. Do not explain familiar developer terms to a specialist audience.

### Editing without flattening the voice

Identify the point and the personal signals worth protecting before rewriting: vocabulary, cadence, humour, uncertainty, digressions and level of polish. Make the minimum effective edit. Leave a strong human sentence alone even when a tidier version is available.

Do not force every paragraph into the same shape or make every sentence equally smooth. Longer spoken sentences, brief fragments and changes of pace can stay when they are clear and characteristic. Likewise, do not remove words such as `actually`, `just` or `I think` mechanically; remove them when they are empty, but keep them when they express real emphasis, uncertainty or spoken rhythm.

Watch for structures that create a synthetic sense of insight: repeated “not X, but Y” contrasts, dramatic colon reveals, stacked fragments, self-answered questions, importance puffery and profound-sounding final lines. State the point directly unless the structure genuinely earns its effect.

## Characteristic moves

These patterns recur in Dean’s strongest writing:

- **Experience → diagnosis → practical conclusion.** Describe what happened, isolate what mattered, then give the reader the useful result.
- **The honest trade-off.** Acknowledge what a solution improves and what it costs.
- **The concrete analogy.** Use a familiar object or situation—the final Lego piece, tiers of information in a meeting—to make an abstract change tangible.
- **The quiet one-line conclusion.** Let a plain sentence carry the point: “And investments require commitment on both sides.”
- **The useful caveat.** Mention version differences, untested paths or conditions exactly where they affect the instruction.
- **The human detail.** A boat that is literally a sauna or a childhood memory can make a larger argument personal without overwhelming it.

Use these moves because the subject calls for them, not as verbal signatures to insert on demand.

## What to avoid

- Generic AI prose: “In today’s rapidly evolving landscape”, “delve into”, “unlock”, “leverage” as a vague verb, “game-changing”, “robust” without a named quality, and tidy three-part conclusions that add nothing.
- Corporate self-promotion, breathless confidence or claims that cannot be demonstrated.
- Long throat-clearing introductions and encyclopedia-style background before the actual problem.
- Repeating the same point in progressively grander language.
- Excessive parallel fragments, rhetorical questions or dramatic one-sentence paragraphs.
- Announcing that a fix is easy instead of making it easy to follow.
- Treating technology names as outcomes.
- False balance. Consider material counterarguments, but do not manufacture one for symmetry.
- Generic closings such as “What do you think?”, “Let me know in the comments” or “I hope you found this useful” unless reader responses are genuinely needed.
- Copying dated surface habits from the archive: casual smileys, repeated “fantastic/brilliant/awesome”, celebratory “Hooray!”, clickbait numbers, or chatty filler around a small tip.
- Sanding away all personality. Clean prose should still sound like a person who has done the work and cares about the result.

## Editing test

Before publishing, ask:

1. Does the opening identify a real problem, experience or tension?
2. Is it clear why Dean is the person telling this story?
3. Are important claims supported by experience, evidence or concrete examples?
4. Does the reader understand the reasoning and the trade-offs?
5. Is the confidence level honest?
6. Could jargon, promotional language or throat-clearing be removed?
7. Do short paragraphs and fragments mark real emphasis rather than imitate a style?
8. Does the ending leave the reader with a useful result, clear position or resonant question?
9. Does every sentence sound natural when read aloud in Dean’s voice?
10. Is the piece concise without omitting context the reader needs?
11. Has the edit made any fact, relationship, feeling or degree of certainty stronger than the source material supports?
12. In a personal piece, does the imagery come from the lived scene rather than decoration added to make the prose sound literary?

For published posts, also follow the SEO, front matter, linking, image and validation requirements in `AGENTS.md`. Those requirements override stylistic preferences where necessary.
